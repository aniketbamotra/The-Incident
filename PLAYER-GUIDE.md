# The Incident — How to Play

Welcome. For one evening you're not yourself — you're a character with a secret,
a goal, and a fuzzy memory of what happened. The whole game runs on your phone
and in the room. Here's everything you need to know. It takes 3 minutes to read.

---

## The idea

Something happened tonight. Nobody has the full picture — everyone holds a
fragment, and some of those fragments contradict each other. The game isn't
about *solving* a fixed mystery. It's about what you choose to **reveal, hide,
or lie about** when you're handed information that could help you or expose you.

You'll talk to people in person. The app is your private brain: your secret,
your memories, the clues only you receive, and the choices only you can make.

---

## Getting in

1. **Scan your QR code** (or open the link / enter the game code your host gives
   you).
2. **Type your name** and tap **Join**.
3. You'll get a **character reveal** — tap through it slowly:
   - Your **name and role**
   - Your **secret** (only you ever see this)
   - Your **objective** (what you're secretly trying to achieve)
   - Three **memories** — things you think you remember
   - **Who to find** — one person you've been told to seek out, and a question
     to ask them
4. Tap **"I've read this"** to enter the game.

> **Example reveal**
> *You are **Vera**, the Fixer.*
> *Secret: you owe the host money, and tonight was the deadline.*
> *Objective: leave before midnight without anyone learning about the debt.*
> *Find **Cass** and ask: "Were you near the study when the lights went out?"*

---

## Your five tabs

**You** — your character sheet: persona, secret, objective, memories, and who to
find. Always here when you need a reminder.

**Events** — the shared story board. Anyone can **add what they witnessed** (a
time, what happened, and who was there). Everyone sees every addition live. Use
it to build a public account of the night — or to muddy it.

**Trust** — rate every other player from 0 to 100. **These ratings are public** —
everyone can see what you think of them, and what they think of you. Your own
trust score is the average of what others have rated you.

**Intel** — public announcements appear here as the night unfolds. This is also
where a **private clue** shows up when one is meant for you ("You have a private
clue. Tap to view.").

**Tips** — send an **anonymous** tip to everyone or to one specific person. No
one ever sees who sent it. Spread a rumor, plant a doubt, or quietly help someone.

---

## Your memories fade

The three things you "remember" don't stay sharp. Over the evening each memory
**decays** — the bar shrinks and the text dims. Once a memory drops below ~40%,
it'll tell you: *"You're no longer certain about this."*

That's intentional. The detail you were dead sure of at 9pm might be a hazy maybe
by 11. Use your certainty while you have it — and be careful how confidently you
repeat something late in the night.

---

## Private clues — the big moment

Sometimes a clue is handed to **you and exactly one other person**, each getting
a slightly different version. When it lands, your screen shows:

- **What the group was told** (the public version everyone sees)
- A note that **someone else also received a version** of this clue
- **Your private clue** — the part only you saw
- **Your situation** — why this is awkward for you specifically

Then you choose:

- **Reveal** — share your version with the whole group. It's posted, **with your
  name on it.**
- **Sit on it** — keep it to yourself. *But the system records that you did.*

Here's the thing: **the group already knows a clue dropped, and that two people
got it.** So hiding is never invisible.

> **Example clue**
> *The group is told: "A glass broke near the study around nine."*
> *Cass also received a version.*
> *Your private clue: "You felt the glass slip from your own hand."*
>
> **If you Reveal:** everyone learns you were at the study — honest, but it puts
> you in the frame.
> **If you Sit on it:** nobody hears it from you... unless Cass reveals their
> version and it contradicts your silence. Then it looks deliberate.

**How the group sees it** (you won't be told which case happened — you just see
what gets posted):
- Both of you reveal → both versions appear, and any contradiction is plain to
  see.
- One reveals, one hides → only the revealed version is posted.
- Both hide → a neutral version quietly appears with **no name attached**.

Honesty is safer than it feels — but sometimes a secret is worth the risk.

---

## When the group gathers

A banner will appear: **"The group is gathering. Go to the main room."** Head
back, talk it out, and tap *"I'm there"* to dismiss it.

## Voting

At certain points the host opens a vote. A full-screen prompt asks **"Who do you
suspect most right now?"** Pick someone and submit. You can **change your vote**
until the host closes it, and your individual vote stays anonymous during the
game.

---

## The finale

At the end, the host puts a screen up for everyone to watch. It reveals, in order:

1. **What actually happened** — the real truth behind every clue.
2. **Every lie** — who got what, who revealed, who hid, and where accounts
   contradicted.
3. **The trust map** — who people *said* they trusted vs who was *actually*
   honest.
4. **Epilogues** — a short story for each player: what you did, who you trusted,
   whether you hit your objective, and whether your secret survived.
5. **The leaderboard** — final scores.

**How scoring leans:** you're rewarded for **revealing honestly** and for
**reading people accurately** (trusting the honest, suspecting the right
person). **Hiding clues costs you.** So a smooth liar can win the room socially
but rarely tops the board — and the finale notices more than you'd expect about
who you talked to and avoided.

---

## Quick etiquette

- **Stay in character.** Lie freely — *as your character.* That's the game.
- **Talk in person.** The app holds your secrets; the drama happens face to face.
- **Don't show your screen.** Your secret, clues, and choices are yours alone.
- **Commit to your objective.** Chasing it is what makes you interesting.

That's it. Trust carefully. Reveal wisely. See you at the finale.
